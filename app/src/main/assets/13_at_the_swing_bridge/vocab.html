Vocabulary

an open return (n)

A return train ticket that lets you to make your return journey on any train, usually within one month.

I didn’t know when I was going to come back so I bought an open return. It was more expensive than a day return.

outbound (adj / adv)

Your journey from where you start to your destination. The opposite of ‘return’ journey.

My outbound journey was delayed, but coming back was fine.

a concession (n)

discount, price reduction

There are concessions available for children. Children under 5 travel for free.

advance (adj)

Something done or prepared before the event or before it is used.

Advance tickets are always cheaper than tickets you buy on the day that you travel.

indicated (adj)

Shown, written on, pointed out

The price, time and seat were all indicated on my ticket.

to alight (v)

To get off a train or bus

To get to the British Library you have to alight at Kings Cross St Pancras.

an allocated seat (n)

A seat that’s reserved and specified on your ticket or booking.

The train was really busy and lots of people were standing. Fortunately, I had an allocated seat so I could sit down.

a replacement bus service (n)

A bus that takes you on your journey when the train is not running, often when there are engineering works.

There’s a replacement bus service between Bletchley and Milton Keynes because of repairs to the train line.

an itinerary (n)

A planned route or journey. Also a document that shows your route or journey.

It looks like I have a very complicated itinerary – I’m going to have to take a lot of different trains.

significant (adj)

Big or important

Tickets are 30% cheaper with a railcard, which is a significant saving.

a regional train (n)

Trains within a limited part of the country, for example, the South East region.

A Network Railcard isn’t valid in all of the country – only on regional trains in South East England.
